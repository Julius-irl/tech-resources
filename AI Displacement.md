# Tech Jobs Ranked by AI Displacement Risk
### From Most Likely to be Replaced → Most AI-Resistant

**How to read this:** Jobs are grouped into 5 tiers based on how soon and how completely AI is likely to displace them. This is not about whether AI will *touch* a role, but *when* AI will touch it. This is about whether the job itself survives or disappears.

**The core principle driving every ranking:**
- **High risk = closed-loop, pattern-based work with clean inputs.** If the task can be described as a runbook, checklist, or repeatable process, AI can do it.
- **Low risk = open-loop, adversarial, accountable, or judgment-based work.** If the job requires deciding what the right question even is, owning the outcome when something goes wrong, or operating in an environment that changes faster than AI can be trained on, humans stay.

**Displacement ≠ elimination.** Most roles don't disappear, they shrink in headcount and shift in what the remaining humans actually do. "High risk" means the role as it exists today will look very different by 2030, with far fewer people doing it.

## What This Means for Your Career

**The three properties that protect any role from AI displacement:**

1. **Adversarial thinking** — Roles that involve defending against or reasoning about human attackers, adversaries, or novel problems evolve faster than AI training cycles. Security, offensive research, and threat hunting all benefit from this.
2. **Accountability** — Someone has to own outcomes when things go wrong. Legal liability, regulatory compliance, and organizational accountability anchor humans in roles where consequences are real. AI can't be sued, fired, or held responsible.
3. **Context-richness** — Every organization's environment, threat model, culture, and constraints are unique and largely undocumented. AI has no organization specific context. Roles that require deeply knowing a specific environment are protected.

**The general principle:** Build toward Tier 4 and 5 skills. Tier 1 and 2 roles are not bad starting points. They are how most people enter tech, but they should be treated as stepping stones, not destinations. The skills that make you AI-resistant (judgment, systems thinking, adversarial reasoning, accountability) are also the skills that make you senior.
